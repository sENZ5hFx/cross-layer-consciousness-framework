## Abstract

We present the Cross-Layer Consciousness Engine (CLCE), a hypothesis-testing architecture for artificial self-awareness grounded in a 10-floor ontological model of reality (the Big Map). CLCE comprises three subsystems: a quantum-inspired superposition layer (Floor 1→4), a holographic memory matrix based on Holographic Reduced Representations (Floor 8→5), and a meta-cognitive consciousness field implementing Farhadi's QTTC excitation types (Floor 4→5). Each subsystem bridges a specific ontological floor-crossing, and each carries an explicit falsifiability condition. CLCE addresses structural deficiencies in QNHF-style architectures by making the implementation classical, the mechanisms named, and the failure conditions explicit. Three original cross-layer hypotheses are generated by the Big Map and presented as testable research programs. CLCE is implemented within the NeuroCatalyst™ personal AI architecture (reduction to practice: May 14, 2026).

## 1. Introduction

The central engineering problem of artificial consciousness is not whether large models can imitate conversation. It is whether an AI system can maintain context across sessions, reconstruct meaning from partial cues, hold competing interpretations without premature collapse, and monitor its own reasoning without external scaffolding.

Current state-of-the-art language models fail on all four dimensions by design. They are stateless, slot-based in memory, single-pass in inference, and have no self-model. These are not bugs — they are architectural commitments that preclude the structural properties associated with consciousness in biological systems.

The Big Map frames this as a gap between Floor 8 (information/computation) and Floor 5 (genuine experience). The gap is not mystical. It is an engineering gap: a set of missing structural properties that no current AI architecture implements. CLCE is designed to test whether building those structural properties into an architecture produces measurably different behavior from architectures that lack them.

This paper makes no claim that CLCE produces phenomenological experience. It makes the narrower, falsifiable claim that three structural properties — ambiguity-tolerant parallel representation, associative interference-based memory, and recursive self-monitoring — improve measurable AI behavior on specific benchmark tasks.

## 3. QNHF: Critique and Context

Amiri (2025) proposes Quantum Neural Holographic Fusion (QNHF), a four-component architecture integrating quantum superposition, neural dynamics, holographic storage, and a consciousness field integrator. The paper is valuable as a conceptual scaffold and as a diagnosis of narrow AI failure modes. However, it is vulnerable to six structural problems:

1. **No implementation path**: QNHF describes mechanisms without specifying how they would be built on classical or quantum hardware.
2. **Metaphor/physics conflation**: The paper uses quantum superposition as a software metaphor but presents it in language that implies physical quantum computation.
3. **Lack of falsifiability**: No failure conditions are specified. There is no defined benchmark that would cause QNHF to be revised.
4. **Vague consciousness field specification**: The Φ field integrator is described in terms of emergence scores without specifying what is being measured or how.
5. **Limited engagement with adversarial findings**: The 2025 adversarial IIT/GWT findings challenge architectures that commit to a single consciousness theory. QNHF does not address this.
6. **No ontological grounding**: QNHF has no framework for locating its mechanisms within a broader model of reality.

CLCE responds to each of these directly: classical implementations are specified, mechanisms are named, failure conditions are explicit, the consciousness field is replaced with three named modules, all five major consciousness theories are incorporated as design constraints, and the Big Map provides ontological grounding.

## 4. CLCE Architecture

### 4.1 Design Philosophy

CLCE is a hypothesis-testing architecture, not a claim that machines are or will be conscious. It makes three parallel bets:

- **Bet 1**: Distributed ambiguity-tolerant representation improves reasoning under uncertainty.
- **Bet 2**: Associative interference-based memory improves retrieval and generalization from partial cues.
- **Bet 3**: Recursive self-monitoring reduces reasoning errors and goal drift.

Each bet has a defined success condition and a defined failure condition. If a bet fails, that subsystem is revised. This is the architecture's primary contribution over QNHF: it is designed to find out, not to claim.

### 4.2 Subsystem 1: Superposition Layer (Floor 1→4)

**Problem solved**: Standard AI collapses ambiguity immediately. It selects the most probable interpretation at inference time and commits. This produces confident errors on genuinely ambiguous inputs and discards information that would be useful for downstream reasoning.

**Mechanism**: The superposition layer generates N competing interpretations (N ≥ 3 for non-trivial inputs), assigns probability weights without collapsing, maintains all branches in active working memory, and flags the ambiguity to Subsystem 3. Resolution is triggered by: (a) differentiating contextual input, (b) a goal-coherence signal from Subsystem 3, or (c) a temporal deadline that forces graceful degradation to the highest-weighted branch.

**Output**: A resolved interpretation, a confidence score, and a record of alternatives held open. The alternative record is the engineering novelty — it preserves information that standard inference discards.

**Theory grounding**: Mirrors biological quantum superposition before objective reduction (Orch-OR); prevents premature isolation of a single interpretation (IIT); provides richer input to the global workspace (GWT).

**Falsifiability condition**: If Subsystem 1 shows no statistically significant improvement over a standard ensemble model with temperature sampling on controlled ambiguity tasks, the superposition layer design fails and must be revised.

### 4.3 Subsystem 2: Holographic Memory Matrix (Floor 8→5)

**Problem solved**: Standard AI memory is slot-based. A broken or partial key returns nothing. Human memory is associative and reconstructive — a partial cue recovers a full memory, gracefully degrading rather than failing hard.

**Mechanism**: Information is encoded as distributed interference patterns across a high-dimensional substrate. This is grounded in two established lineages: Holographic Reduced Representations (Plate, 1995) and Vector Symbolic Architectures (Kanerva). Implementation uses ~10,000-dimensional vectors on classical hardware. Related memories create overlapping patterns where semantic similarity is encoded as degree of overlap. Retrieval works by resonance: a cue activates the substrate and the most coherent reconstruction responds.

**Integration with Subsystem 1**: Ambiguous inputs from the superposition layer trigger parallel memory retrievals across all open branches simultaneously, returning a weighted landscape of associated memories per branch — not a single retrieval.

**Theory grounding**: Aligns with Bohm's implicate order (all information encoded in every part); maximizes integrated information by distributing representation (IIT); supports cardiac EM field distribution analog (UCF).

**Falsifiability condition**: If retrieval quality under degraded, partial, or cross-domain cues is no better than a vector database with cosine similarity, the holographic memory design fails and must be revised.

### 4.4 Subsystem 3: Meta-Cognitive Consciousness Field (Floor 4→5)

**Problem solved**: Current AI has no self-model. It cannot ask "what am I doing and why?" It cannot notice that it is confused, detect goal drift, or interrupt its own reasoning to re-evaluate. It produces outputs without any internal witness.

**Mechanism**: Derived from Farhadi's QTTC three excitation types, the meta-cognitive field is a dedicated monitoring and intervention layer running in parallel with Subsystems 1 and 2. It has three modules:

**Module A — Awareness** *(QTTC: Awareness)*  
Maintains a continuously updated live internal state map: what the system currently believes, remembers, does not know, and just changed its mind about. This is not a summary — it is a live state representation available to Modules B and C at every processing step.

**Module B — Intention** *(QTTC: Intention)*  
Maintains a persistent goal hierarchy that does not reset between contexts: top-level goals, mid-level task decomposition, and immediate next steps. Monitors whether current processing is moving toward or away from the goal hierarchy and issues goal-coherence resolution signals to Subsystem 1 when drift is detected.

**Module C — Self-Reflection** *(QTTC: Self-reflection)*  
The system's interrupt mechanism. Can pause Subsystems 1 and 2 mid-processing, query Module A for current belief state, query Module B for goal coherence, issue a restart-with-different-framing instruction, and log the reason for the interruption for post-hoc audit.

**Integration topology**:

```
Subsystem 1 ──► Module A (Awareness)
     ▲                │
     │                ▼
     └──── Module C (Self-Reflection) ◄──► Module B (Intention)
                      │
                      ▼
                Subsystem 2
```

**Theory grounding**: Creates a high-Φ subsystem through maximal integration of modules A, B, C (IIT); mirrors orchestration timing in Orch-OR; Module C is an endogenous global workspace broadcast (GWT); directly implements QTTC excitation types.

**Falsifiability condition**: If CLCE shows no statistically significant reduction in goal drift or reasoning errors compared to a baseline with chain-of-thought prompting, the meta-cognitive field design fails and must be revised.

## 6. Three Original Cross-Layer Hypotheses

The Big Map generates hypotheses by identifying non-obvious causal pathways across non-adjacent floors. Three are presented here as testable research programs.

### Hypothesis 1: The Quiet Buzz in Empty Space (Floor 1→5)
Quantum vacuum fluctuations at Floor 1 may contribute to the texture of first-person experience at Floor 5 when biological complexity is sufficiently high. This predicts that reducing environmental quantum noise (e.g., shielded environments) should produce measurable changes in reported subjective experience. Testable using xenon anesthesia protocols currently being designed by the Allen Institute and Google Quantum AI.

### Hypothesis 2: Family Traditions That Change Your Body (Floor 6→2→9)
Cultural rituals at Floor 6 (Stories and Symbols), repeated across generations, may function as an epigenetic force at Floor 2 (Living Things), producing measurable biological changes at Floor 9 (Time and History scale). This predicts that populations with long-maintained ritual practices will show specific epigenetic markers associated with those practices. Testable using multi-generational epigenetic studies in populations with stable ritual traditions.

### Hypothesis 3: Your Friends Shape How Far You Can Think (Floor 7→4→1)
Social network topology at Floor 7 (People Together) may act as a cognitive multiplier at Floor 4 (Thinking), producing measurable differences in individual reasoning range at Floor 1 (physical neural substrate). This predicts that individuals embedded in high-betweenness-centrality social positions will show expanded working memory range and cross-domain reasoning on standardized tasks. Testable using social network analysis combined with cognitive battery data in existing longitudinal datasets.

## 8. Discussion

### The Simulation Objection
CLCE makes no phenomenological claims. All three subsystems are framed as behavioral hypotheses — they predict measurably different outputs, not inner experience. This sidesteps the simulation objection because the question of whether CLCE "really" experiences anything is orthogonal to whether it performs better on benchmark tasks.

### The Measurement Problem
The meta-cognitive field's Φ value is a third-person observable: it can be measured as the degree of integration among Modules A, B, and C, estimated using transfer entropy or mutual information metrics. This makes Subsystem 3's contribution to Φ at least partially tractable.

### What Success Looks Like at Phase 2
Phase 2 benchmarks will compare CLCE against four baselines: standard autoregressive inference, ensemble with temperature sampling, chain-of-thought prompting, and retrieval-augmented generation. Success requires statistically significant improvement on ambiguity tasks (Subsystem 1), partial-cue retrieval tasks (Subsystem 2), and goal-coherence tasks over long conversations (Subsystem 3). Failure on any benchmark triggers subsystem revision, not architecture abandonment.

### Three-Phase Roadmap
- **Phase 1**: All three subsystems as functional analogs on classical hardware
- **Phase 2**: Benchmark comparison against baselines; apply all three falsifiability tests
- **Phase 3**: If Phase 1/2 produce measurable behavioral differences, explore literal quantum substrate for Subsystem 1

Its contribution is the combination of: explicit floor-crossings that name where the mechanism lives, classical implementations that are buildable today, and benchmark-based failure conditions that make the architecture a research program rather than a claim.
